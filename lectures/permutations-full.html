Module 06: Complete Theory

Permutations and Combinations

← Return to Modules

1. Fundamental Counting Principles

Multiplication Rule: If an event can occur in m ways and another independent event can occur in n ways, then both events together can occur in m × n ways.
Addition Rule: If an event can occur in m ways or another mutually exclusive event can occur in n ways, then either event can occur in m + n ways.

Example 1.1:

A restaurant offers 3 appetizers, 5 main courses, and 4 desserts. How many complete meals are possible?

Solution: Using the Multiplication Rule:

Total meals = 3 × 5 × 4 = 60 different complete meals

2. Permutations

Definition 2.1: A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order. The number of permutations of n distinct objects taken r at a time is denoted P(n, r) or nPr.

Formula: P(n, r) = n!/(n-r)!

Types of Permutations

  • Linear Permutations: P(n, n) = n! - all n objects arranged
  • Partial Permutations: P(n, r) - r objects from n arranged
  • Circular Permutations: (n-1)! - arrangements in a circle
  • With Repetition: nr - when objects can be reused

Example 2.1: Linear Permutation

How many ways can 5 books be arranged on a shelf?

P(5, 5) = 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120 ways

Example 2.2: Partial Permutation

A race has 10 runners. In how many ways can gold, silver, and bronze medals be awarded?

P(10, 3) = 10!/(10-3)! = 10!/7! = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720 ways

Example 2.3: Circular Permutation

In how many ways can 6 people be seated around a circular table?

Circular permutations = (6-1)! = 5! = 120 ways

(We fix one person's position to eliminate rotational symmetry)

3. Permutations with Restrictions

Common Restriction Types:

  • Identical Objects: n!/n₁!n₂!...nₖ! where nᵢ are counts of each type
  • Adjacent Objects: Treat as a single unit, then multiply by internal arrangements
  • Non-Adjacent Objects: Arrange others first, then place restricted objects in gaps
  • Fixed Positions: Fill fixed positions first, then arrange remaining

Example 3.1: Identical Objects

How many distinct arrangements of the letters in "MISSISSIPPI"?

Total letters = 11: M(1), I(4), S(4), P(2)

Arrangements = 11!/(1!×4!×4!×2!) = 34,650

Example 3.2: Adjacent Restriction

How many ways can 5 people stand in a line if 2 specific people must stand together?

Solution:

1. Treat the 2 people as one unit: 4 units to arrange = 4! = 24 ways

2. The 2 people within their unit can swap: 2! = 2 ways

Total = 24 × 2 = 48 ways

Example 3.3: Non-Adjacent Restriction

How many ways can 3 boys and 4 girls stand in a line if no two boys are adjacent?

Solution:

1. Arrange 4 girls: 4! = 24 ways

2. This creates 5 gaps: _G_G_G_G_

3. Place 3 boys in 5 gaps: P(5, 3) = 60 ways

Total = 24 × 60 = 1,440 ways

4. Combinations

Definition 4.1: A combination is a selection of objects without regard to order. The number of combinations of n objects taken r at a time is denoted C(n, r) or nCr.

Formula: C(n, r) = n!/(r!(n-r)!)

Key Difference: Permutations vs Combinations

Permutations: Order matters - ABC ≠ BAC

Combinations: Order doesn't matter - {A,B,C} = {B,A,C}

Relationship: P(n, r) = C(n, r) × r!

Example 4.1:

A committee of 4 people is to be formed from 10 candidates. How many committees are possible?

C(10, 4) = 10!/(4!×6!) = (10×9×8×7)/(4×3×2×1) = 210

Example 4.2: Multiple Groups

From 15 students, select 3 for debate, 4 for drama, and 5 for music (students can't overlap).

Solution:

C(15, 3) × C(12, 4) × C(8, 5)

= 455 × 495 × 56 = 12,612,600 ways

5. Combinations with Restrictions

Example 5.1: At Least/At Most

From 8 men and 7 women, select a committee of 5 with at least 2 women.

Solution: Count cases with 2, 3, 4, or 5 women:

• 2 women, 3 men: C(7,2)×C(8,3) = 21×56 = 1,176

• 3 women, 2 men: C(7,3)×C(8,2) = 35×28 = 980

• 4 women, 1 man: C(7,4)×C(8,1) = 35×8 = 280

• 5 women, 0 men: C(7,5)×C(8,0) = 21×1 = 21

Total = 1,176 + 980 + 280 + 21 = 2,457

Example 5.2: Complementary Counting

Same problem using complement: at least 2 women = total - (0 women or 1 woman)

Total committees: C(15, 5) = 3,003

0 women (5 men): C(8, 5) = 56

1 woman (4 men): C(7,1)×C(8,4) = 7×70 = 490

With at least 2 women = 3,003 - 56 - 490 = 2,457 ✓

6. Advanced Topics

Combinations with Repetition

Formula: C(n+r-1, r) - selecting r items from n types with repetition allowed

Example: How many ways to select 4 fruits from apples, oranges, and bananas?

C(3+4-1, 4) = C(6, 4) = 15 ways

Distributing Identical Objects

Stars and Bars Method: Distribute n identical objects into k bins

Formula: C(n+k-1, k-1)

Example: Distribute 10 identical candies to 4 children:

C(10+4-1, 4-1) = C(13, 3) = 286 ways

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

How many 4-digit numbers can be formed using digits 1-9 without repetition?

Solution

Order matters (permutation): P(9, 4)

= 9!/(9-4)! = 9!/5!

= 9 × 8 × 7 × 6 = 3,024

Answer: 3,024 numbers

Problem 2:

In how many ways can the word "BANANA" be arranged?

Solution

6 letters: B(1), A(3), N(2)

Arrangements = 6!/(1!×3!×2!)

= 720/(1×6×2) = 720/12 = 60

Answer: 60 arrangements

Problem 3:

A pizza shop offers 10 toppings. How many different 3-topping pizzas can be made?

Solution

Order doesn't matter (combination): C(10, 3)

= 10!/(3!×7!) = (10×9×8)/(3×2×1)

= 720/6 = 120

Answer: 120 different pizzas

Problem 4:

How many ways can 7 people be seated in a row if 3 specific people must sit together?

Solution

Treat 3 people as one unit: 5 units to arrange

Arrangements of units: 5! = 120

Internal arrangements of 3 people: 3! = 6

Total = 120 × 6 = 720

Answer: 720 ways

Problem 5:

From a deck of 52 cards, how many 5-card hands contain exactly 3 aces?

Solution

Choose 3 aces from 4: C(4, 3) = 4

Choose 2 non-aces from 48: C(48, 2) = 1,128

Total hands = 4 × 1,128 = 4,512

Answer: 4,512 hands

Problem 6:

How many positive integer solutions exist for x + y + z = 10?

Solution

Using stars and bars with restriction x,y,z ≥ 1:

Let x' = x-1, y' = y-1, z' = z-1 (all ≥ 0)

Then x' + y' + z' = 7

Solutions = C(7+3-1, 3-1) = C(9, 2) = 36

Answer: 36 solutions

Chapter Summary

  1. Multiplication and addition rules are fundamental to counting
  2. Permutations count ordered arrangements: P(n,r) = n!/(n-r)!
  3. Combinations count unordered selections: C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n-r)!)
  4. Restrictions require careful case analysis or complementary counting
  5. Repetition allowed: different formulas for permutations (nr) and combinations (C(n+r-1,r))
  6. Choose the right tool: does order matter? Can objects repeat?
