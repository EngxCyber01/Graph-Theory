Planar Graphs and Euler's Formula

1. Introduction to Planar Graphs

Definition 1.1: A graph G is planar if it can be drawn in the plane such that no two edges cross except at their endpoints.
Definition 1.2: A planar embedding (or plane graph) is a specific drawing of a planar graph in the plane with no crossings.

Key Observations

  • A graph may have multiple different planar embeddings
  • Planarity is a property of the graph, not a specific drawing
  • Edges can be curves, not just straight lines
  • The embedding divides the plane into regions called faces

Example 1.1:

K4 (complete graph on 4 vertices) is planar:

  • Can be drawn as a triangle with one vertex inside
  • Can also be drawn as a tetrahedron projection
  • Has 4 vertices, 6 edges
  • Different embeddings create different face structures

2. Euler's Formula

Theorem 2.1 (Euler's Formula): For a connected planar graph with v vertices, e edges, and f faces:
v - e + f = 2

Proof: By induction on the number of edges.

Base case: Tree with v vertices has e = v-1 edges and f = 1 face (the outer region).
v - e + f = v - (v-1) + 1 = 2 ✓

Inductive step: For graph with cycle, remove edge e from cycle:
  • Vertices unchanged: v' = v
  • Edges decrease: e' = e - 1
  • Faces decrease: f' = f - 1 (two faces merge)
  • By induction: v' - e' + f' = 2
  • So: v - (e-1) + (f-1) = 2 → v - e + f = 2 ∎

Example 2.1:

Cube graph:

  • v = 8 vertices
  • e = 12 edges
  • f = ? faces
  • By Euler: 8 - 12 + f = 2 → f = 6 faces ✓
  • (Matches 6 faces of a cube!)

Example 2.2:

Planar embedding of K4:

  • v = 4, e = 6
  • f = 2 - v + e = 2 - 4 + 6 = 4 faces
  • Includes the outer unbounded face
Definition 2.1: The degree of a face is the number of edges bordering it (counting each edge once or twice if it borders the face on both sides).
Theorem 2.2: For a connected planar graph: Σ deg(fi) = 2e

Proof: Each edge is counted exactly twice in the sum of face degrees (once for each side), similar to the Handshaking Lemma. ∎

3. Bounds on Planar Graphs

Theorem 3.1: For a connected planar graph with v ≥ 3 vertices and e edges:
e ≤ 3v - 6

Proof:
  • Each face has degree ≥ 3 (minimum: triangle)
  • So: Σ deg(fi) ≥ 3f
  • By Theorem 2.2: 2e ≥ 3f
  • By Euler: f = 2 - v + e
  • Substituting: 2e ≥ 3(2 - v + e)
  • 2e ≥ 6 - 3v + 3e
  • -e ≥ 6 - 3v
  • e ≤ 3v - 6 ∎

Example 3.1: Using the bound to prove non-planarity

Is K5 planar?

  • K5 has v = 5, e = 10
  • If planar: e ≤ 3v - 6 = 3(5) - 6 = 9
  • But 10 > 9, contradiction!
  • K5 is NOT planar
Theorem 3.2: For a connected planar graph with v ≥ 3 vertices, e edges, and no triangles:
e ≤ 2v - 4

Proof: Similar to Theorem 3.1, but each face has degree ≥ 4. ∎

Example 3.2:

Is K3,3 planar?

  • K3,3: v = 6, e = 9
  • K3,3 is bipartite → no odd cycles → no triangles
  • If planar with no triangles: e ≤ 2v - 4 = 2(6) - 4 = 8
  • But 9 > 8, contradiction!
  • K3,3 is NOT planar
Corollary 3.1: Every planar graph has a vertex with degree ≤ 5.

Proof:
  • If all vertices had degree ≥ 6: Σ deg(v) ≥ 6v
  • By Handshaking: 2e = Σ deg(v) ≥ 6v
  • So: e ≥ 3v
  • But for planar: e ≤ 3v - 6 < 3v, contradiction!
  • Therefore, some vertex has degree ≤ 5 ∎

4. Kuratowski's Theorem

Definition 4.1: A subdivision of a graph is obtained by replacing edges with paths (inserting vertices of degree 2).
Theorem 4.1 (Kuratowski, 1930): A graph is planar if and only if it contains no subgraph that is a subdivision of K5 or K3,3.

Interpretation

  • K5 and K3,3 are the "fundamental" non-planar graphs
  • Any non-planar graph contains one of these (possibly with extra vertices)
  • This gives a characterization of planarity
  • Can be used as basis for planarity testing algorithms

Example 4.1:

Petersen graph is non-planar because it contains K3,3 subdivision:

  • Has 10 vertices, 15 edges
  • Check: 15 ≤ 3(10) - 6 = 24 ✓ (bound doesn't prove non-planarity)
  • But contains K3,3 subdivision
  • By Kuratowski: NOT planar

5. Applications of Planar Graphs

Circuit Board Design

  • Electronic components as vertices
  • Connections as edges
  • Planar layout avoids wire crossings (reduces costs)
  • Multi-layer boards allow non-planar circuits

Geographic Maps

  • Countries/regions as vertices
  • Adjacency as edges
  • Map graphs are always planar
  • Leads to Four Color Theorem
Four Color Theorem: Every planar graph can be vertex-colored with at most 4 colors such that adjacent vertices have different colors.

(Proved by Appel and Haken, 1976, using computer assistance)

6. Duality in Planar Graphs

Definition 6.1: The dual graph G* of a planar graph G is constructed by:
  • Placing a vertex in each face of G
  • For each edge e in G, connecting the vertices in the faces on either side of e

Properties of Dual Graphs

  • v* = f (vertices in dual = faces in primal)
  • f* = v (faces in dual = vertices in primal)
  • e* = e (same number of edges)
  • (G*)* = G (dual of dual is original)

Example 6.1:

Cube graph (v=8, e=12, f=6) has dual:

  • Octahedron (v*=6, e*=12, f*=8)
  • Vertices and faces are swapped
  • Both satisfy Euler's formula

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

A connected planar graph has 12 vertices and 20 edges. How many faces does it have?

Solution

Using Euler's formula: v - e + f = 2

12 - 20 + f = 2

f = 10

Answer: 10 faces

Problem 2:

Show that K6 (complete graph on 6 vertices) is not planar.

Solution

K6 has v = 6, e = C(6,2) = 15

For planar graph: e ≤ 3v - 6 = 3(6) - 6 = 12

Since 15 > 12, K6 violates the bound

K6 is NOT planar

Problem 3:

In a planar embedding, the sum of face degrees is 30. How many edges does the graph have?

Solution

By Theorem 2.2: Σ deg(fi) = 2e

30 = 2e

e = 15

Answer: 15 edges

Problem 4:

Prove that every planar graph with at least 5 vertices has at least two vertices of degree ≤ 5.

Solution

By Corollary 3.1: at least one vertex v has deg(v) ≤ 5

Remove v from graph, get graph G'

G' is still planar with v' ≥ 4 vertices

If v' ≥ 3: apply Corollary 3.1 again

G' has vertex u with deg(u) ≤ 5

Both v and u in original graph have degree ≤ 5 ∎

Problem 5:

A tree with 10 vertices is drawn in the plane. How many faces does it have?

Solution

Tree with v = 10 has e = 9 edges

Euler's formula: v - e + f = 2

10 - 9 + f = 2

f = 1

Answer: 1 face (only the outer face)

Trees have no enclosed regions!

Problem 6:

Can a planar graph have 8 vertices where each vertex has degree 6?

Solution

If all 8 vertices have degree 6:

Σ deg(v) = 8 × 6 = 48

By Handshaking: 2e = 48 → e = 24

For planar: e ≤ 3v - 6 = 3(8) - 6 = 18

But 24 > 18, contradiction!

Answer: No, impossible

Problem 7:

Find the number of edges in the dual graph of a cube.

Solution

Cube: v = 8, e = 12, f = 6

Dual graph property: e* = e

Answer: 12 edges

(The dual is an octahedron)

Problem 8:

Prove that in a planar graph, if all faces have degree ≥ 4, then e ≤ 2f - 4.

Solution

Given: each face degree ≥ 4

Σ deg(fi) ≥ 4f

By Theorem 2.2: 2e ≥ 4f

So: e ≥ 2f

From Euler: f = 2 - v + e

Substitute into e ≥ 2f:

e ≥ 2(2 - v + e)

e ≥ 4 - 2v + 2e

-e ≥ 4 - 2v

e ≤ 2v - 4 ∎

Chapter Summary

  1. Planar graphs can be drawn without edge crossings
  2. Euler's formula: v - e + f = 2 (connected planar)
  3. Edge bound: e ≤ 3v - 6 for v ≥ 3
  4. K5 and K3,3 are non-planar
  5. Kuratowski's Theorem characterizes planarity
  6. Every planar graph has vertex with degree ≤ 5
  7. Applications: circuit design, maps, Four Color Theorem
