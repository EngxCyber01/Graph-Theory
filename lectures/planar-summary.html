Module 03: Quick Review

Planar Graphs at a Glance

← Return to Modules

Key Definition

Planar Graph: Can be drawn in the plane with no edge crossings

Euler's Formula

v - e + f = 2

Where: v = vertices, e = edges, f = faces

Works for any connected planar graph!

Key Theorems

Theorem 1: If G is planar with v ≥ 3:
e ≤ 3v - 6
Theorem 2: If G is planar with no triangles:
e ≤ 2v - 4
Kuratowski's Theorem: G is planar ⟺ it contains no K5 or K3,3 subdivision

Famous Non-Planar Graphs

  • K5: Complete graph on 5 vertices (10 edges)
  • K3,3: Complete bipartite graph (9 edges)

Quick Tests for Planarity

  1. Check if e ≤ 3v - 6 (necessary but not sufficient)
  2. Look for K5 or K3,3 subdivisions
  3. Try to draw without crossings

Quick Examples

  • K4: Planar (can draw as tetrahedron)
  • K5: NOT planar (10 > 3(5)-6 = 9)
  • Trees: Always planar
  • Cycles: Always planar
  • Cube graph: Planar (8 vertices, 12 edges)
Sum of face degrees = 2e

Quick Problem

Q: Planar graph with 6 vertices, 10 edges. How many faces?

A: v - e + f = 2 → 6 - 10 + f = 2 → f = 6 faces

Key Takeaways

  1. Euler's formula: v - e + f = 2 (connected planar)
  2. Edge bound: e ≤ 3v - 6
  3. K5 and K3,3 are the fundamental non-planar graphs
  4. Planarity has applications in circuit design, maps
